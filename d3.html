About

Maui Island, Hawaii, offers warm, clear Pacific waters perfect for diving, with visibility up to 100 feet. Its reefs host green sea turtles (honu) and hawksbill turtles, often seen basking or feeding on algae. Water temperatures range from 24-28°C, ideal for year-round diving. Popular spots like Molokini Crater provide sheltered dives with gentle currents, while Turtle Town guarantees frequent turtle encounters, especially along the south coast.

History

Maui’s history stretches back to Polynesian settlers arriving around 400 AD, establishing a rich cultural base. Unified under King Kamehameha I in 1795, it became a hub for whaling in the 19th century. The 20th century brought tourism, with Haleakalā National Park established in 1961. Today, it balances historical preservation with a thriving dive tourism industry, honoring its volcanic origins.

Cultural Highlights

Maui’s culture is rooted in Hawaiian traditions, with hula dances and lei-making reflecting Polynesian heritage. The East Maui Taro Festival celebrates native agriculture, while luaus offer feasts of kalua pork and poi. Modern influences include the Maui Arts & Cultural Center, hosting global performances. The island’s aloha spirit shines through community events and respect for nature.

Must-Visits

Dive at Molokini Crater for pristine reefs and turtle sightings. Visit Turtle Town near Wailea for honu basking spots. Haleakalā National Park offers sunrise hikes and volcanic landscapes. The Road to Hana features waterfalls and black sand beaches like Wai’anapanapa. Lahaina town provides historical charm with its whaling museum and waterfront.
